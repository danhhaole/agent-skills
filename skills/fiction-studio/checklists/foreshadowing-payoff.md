# Foreshadowing & Payoff Checklist

Run after the outline (Phase 1) to plan deliberate setups, and again before the
final polish (Phase 9) to confirm none were dropped — as Aristotle. The principle
is Chekhov's gun: a promise made to the reader (a planted detail, an unanswered
question, a skill shown) creates an expectation that must eventually be honoured.
Orphaned setups feel like loose threads; un-set-up payoffs feel like cheating.

## Setups → payoffs (nothing planted is wasted)
- [ ] Every significant planted detail (object, skill, secret, prophecy, wound)
      pays off later — or is deliberately a red herring with its own purpose.
- [ ] Each major reveal/twist was fairly set up earlier, so it lands as
      "inevitable in hindsight," not arbitrary.
- [ ] Foreshadowing is subtle enough to miss on first read, clear enough to
      satisfy on reread — not a billboard, not invisible.
- [ ] The climax's decisive tool/lesson/ally was established before it's needed
      (no last-minute rescue the reader couldn't anticipate).

## Open questions → answers
- [ ] Every dramatic question the book raises is answered or left open *on purpose*.
- [ ] Subplots resolve or are intentionally left to a sequel (and that's signalled).
- [ ] No character vanishes mid-book without explanation.
- [ ] Mysteries the reader is invited to puzzle over get a fair resolution.

## Theme & motif
- [ ] Recurring images/motifs build toward meaning rather than just repeating.
- [ ] The theme stated/implied early is paid off by the ending's events.

## Track it
- [ ] Keep a running setup→payoff ledger (in the continuity log or scene list):
      each planted promise, where it pays off, and any still unpaid. Unpaid rows
      at Phase 9 are the fix list — pay them off, convert them to red herrings
      with purpose, or cut the setup.
