# Genre Reference

Borges' working file. Each genre is a *contract* with the reader: they opened the
book expecting specific satisfactions. You can deliver them, or break them on
purpose with a better payoff — but breaking one by accident reads as a failure,
not a choice. Use this to run a **genre check** (does the work keep its promise?)
and to advise other members on convention.

## How to use this file
- At Phase 0, name the genre (and sub-genre) and read the author the **Promise**
  and **Pitfalls** so expectations are set before a word is drafted.
- As a QA gate (Borges' `genre check`), walk the genre's checklist against the
  outline or draft; flag each unmet promise as a fix or a deliberate subversion.
- A story can braid genres (romantasy, literary SF). Run both contracts and note
  where they pull against each other — that tension is often the book's engine.

## Fantasy
**Promise:** wonder, a coherent secondary world, a sense of scale, and usually a
rising-power or quest arc with a moral weight.

**Genre check**
- [ ] The world has rules that hold; magic has clear **costs and limits**.
- [ ] Wonder is delivered — at least one image or idea the reader hasn't seen worn flat.
- [ ] Power is earned through struggle, not handed over; no easy escape hatches.
- [ ] Stakes are both personal (the protagonist) and large (the world/order).
- [ ] Lore serves the story; the reader is never buried in exposition to follow it.
- [ ] The ending pays off the central conflict the magic/world set up.

**Pitfalls:** unlimited or inconsistent magic (kills tension); worldbuilding that
smothers character; a chosen-one with no agency; map-and-genealogy info-dumps.

## Science fiction
**Promise:** one or more "what if" premises followed rigorously, and ideas that
land as human consequences — not just gadgets.

**Genre check**
- [ ] The central premise is followed to its logical implications, including the ugly ones.
- [ ] Internal logic is consistent; problems aren't solved by hand-waved new tech.
- [ ] The idea matters *to people* — there's a human/social stake, not only a puzzle.
- [ ] Exposition is dramatized, not lectured.
- [ ] The science/speculation is plausible *within the story's own rules*.

**Pitfalls:** tech that conveniently appears to solve the climax; cold idea with
no human cost; "as you know" exposition dumps; premise abandoned halfway.

## Mystery / crime
**Promise:** a fair-play puzzle and a satisfying, earned reveal — the reader
*could* have solved it, and wants to reread to catch the clues.

**Genre check**
- [ ] Every clue needed for the solution is on the page before the reveal (fair play).
- [ ] Red herrings are fair — misdirection, not cheating.
- [ ] The detective/POV reasons; the solution isn't blind luck or a withheld fact.
- [ ] The culprit was present/possible from early on, not a last-page stranger.
- [ ] The reveal recontextualizes earlier scenes (the "of course!" click).
- [ ] Stakes and tension sustain between clue-drops.

**Pitfalls:** withholding a decisive clue from the reader; solution by coincidence;
the culprit introduced too late; a detective who's handed the answer.

## Thriller
**Promise:** escalating stakes, time pressure, and a competent antagonist —
forward motion that won't let the reader put it down.

**Genre check**
- [ ] Stakes escalate; each beat raises the cost of failure.
- [ ] There is a clock or pressure that forbids the protagonist from resting.
- [ ] The antagonist is genuinely capable — the threat feels real.
- [ ] Scenes end on hooks; tension rarely slackens for rumination.
- [ ] Setbacks are real; the protagonist doesn't win every exchange.
- [ ] The climax is the maximum-pressure point, not an anticlimax.

**Pitfalls:** a flat or incompetent villain; saggy middle with no escalation;
protagonist never truly endangered; tension diluted by digression.

## Romance
**Promise:** two (or more) compelling leads, rising emotional intimacy against
real obstacles, and an emotionally satisfying ending (HEA/HFN is the core vow).

**Genre check**
- [ ] Both leads are vivid and worth rooting for; the reader wants them together.
- [ ] The relationship has a real obstacle — internal (the wound) and/or external.
- [ ] Intimacy escalates in believable beats; attraction is shown, not asserted.
- [ ] The conflict keeping them apart isn't a misunderstanding a sentence would fix.
- [ ] Emotional interiority is present — we feel the longing and the cost.
- [ ] The ending delivers the promised HEA/HFN (or subverts it with full intent).

**Pitfalls:** the "idiot plot" (one honest conversation ends the book); chemistry
told not shown; a third-act breakup with no real cause; an unearned reconciliation.

## Horror
**Promise:** dread and the violation of safety — the reader wants to be afraid,
unsettled, and made to feel vulnerable.

**Genre check**
- [ ] Dread is built *before* the scare; atmosphere does the heavy lifting.
- [ ] The protagonist is vulnerable; the threat outmatches easy solutions.
- [ ] The source of fear has rules the reader can dread (even if unseen).
- [ ] Pacing alternates pressure and release; relentlessness numbs.
- [ ] The horror means something — it preys on a real human fear or theme.
- [ ] The ending doesn't fully neutralize the dread (or does so with intent).

**Pitfalls:** gore substituting for dread; a monster with no rules (un-dreadable);
characters acting stupidly to force scares; the threat explained until it's toothless.

## Literary
**Promise:** voice, theme, and interiority foregrounded; the reward is insight and
language, not plot mechanics.

**Genre check**
- [ ] The prose itself is a pleasure — voice is distinct and controlled.
- [ ] Theme is explored with genuine complexity, not stated as a moral.
- [ ] Character interiority carries the work; change is internal and earned.
- [ ] Subtext and implication do real work; not everything is spelled out.
- [ ] Even a quiet plot has shape — something shifts, a value turns.
- [ ] Ambiguity is purposeful, not evasive.

**Pitfalls:** beautiful sentences with no underlying movement; theme as lecture;
opacity mistaken for depth; a story so quiet nothing changes at all.

## Young adult (YA)
**Promise:** a teen protagonist whose agency drives the story, immediacy of
emotion, and a coming-of-age core — written *to* teens, never down to them.

**Genre check**
- [ ] The protagonist is a teen who *drives* events (adults don't solve the plot).
- [ ] Emotional stakes are immediate and high; first-experience intensity is honoured.
- [ ] Voice is authentic to the age without pandering or dated slang.
- [ ] Themes of identity/becoming are present and taken seriously.
- [ ] Content (violence, sex, language) suits the target age band — and is handled, not gratuitous.
- [ ] Pacing respects a reader who will close a slow book.

**Pitfalls:** adults rescuing the teen; preachiness; a "teen" who reads as 30;
talking down to the reader; trauma used as decoration.

## Historical
**Promise:** an immersive, accurate past that feels lived-in, with characters
whose worldview belongs to their time.

**Genre check**
- [ ] Period detail is accurate and woven in sensory, not researched-at-the-reader.
- [ ] Characters' attitudes and knowledge fit their era (no modern transplants).
- [ ] Anachronisms (objects, language, ideas) are caught and justified or cut.
- [ ] The history pressures the plot; setting isn't mere costume.
- [ ] Real events/figures are handled with care and a note on liberties taken.
- [ ] The voice evokes the period without becoming unreadable to a modern reader.

**Pitfalls:** info-dump research; modern morality/voice in period mouths;
anachronistic detail; setting that could be swapped out with no effect on plot.

## Subversion done right
Breaking a convention works only when the break pays off *better* than meeting it.
The `subvert` move, in three steps:
1. **Name the convention** and the exact expectation the reader holds.
2. **Break it deliberately** — and make the break itself meaningful (thematic,
   character-revealing, or a sharper surprise).
3. **Pay it off** so the reader feels rewarded for trusting you, not cheated.

If you can't name the payoff, you don't have a subversion — you have a broken
promise. Meet the convention instead.
