# Innovation & Disruption — Christensen's Working File

Tools for judging whether an opportunity is real, whether the game is
changing, and how to balance today's business against tomorrow's. Sources
`[S#]` or `(assumption)` throughout.

## Jobs-to-be-done audit

**When:** evaluating a new product/market/technology opportunity; whenever
adoption is assumed rather than argued.

**How:** name the progress the customer is trying to make — functional,
emotional, social — in their words, not the product's. Identify what they
"hire" today (including non-consumption and workarounds — a spreadsheet, an
intern, doing nothing), what that costs them, and what would make them fire
it. Then test: does the proposal do the job *better on the dimensions the
customer measures*, and what must they give up to switch?

**Output format:**

```
## Job audit: [opportunity]
- The job: [one sentence, customer's perspective]
- Hired today: [current solution] — costs them [what] [S#]
- Firing criteria: [what would make them switch]
- Our proposal does the job better at: … / worse at: …
- Switching friction: [habit, integration, risk, price]
- Verdict: [real job & real advantage / real job, weak advantage / manufactured job]
### So what
```

**Tips:** "no one does this today" cuts both ways — non-consumption is the
best disruption soil and the best sign there is no job. The tiebreaker is
whether people already cobble together workarounds.

## Demand evidence & research prescription

**When:** a load-bearing assumption is about customer behaviour — "they will
switch", "they will pay X", "adoption reaches Y%" — and the fact base offers
only secondary sources (analyst reports, vendor claims, analogies).

**How:** the board cannot interview customers, but it can *prescribe* the
research that would settle the question before the money is committed. Grade
what the fact base actually holds: behavioural evidence (what customers
already do — usage data, workarounds paid for, churn from incumbents) beats
stated intent (surveys, "would you buy" answers) beats vendor and analyst
claims. Where the grade is too weak for the money at stake, write the
prescription: who to talk to (8–12 per segment is usually enough to hear the
pattern), the 3–5 JTBD questions to ask (about what they *did* last time,
not what they *would* do), and — decided in advance — what the executive
must hear to proceed and what would kill the thesis.

**Output format:** per assumption — `evidence grade (behavioural / stated /
claimed) → prescription: [who, n, key questions] → proceed signal / kill
signal`. Goes into the assumptions register's "verify by / how" column; big
bets stage the spend behind it (Taleb's cheapen-the-bet).

## Disruption check

**When:** a cheaper/simpler player is appearing below, or the client is
considering being that player.

**How:** plot two trajectories — what the market's mainstream customers need
over time, and what the challenger's offering delivers. Disruption pattern:
the challenger starts below the needs line (dismissible), improves faster,
and crosses it. Check the enablers: a technology or model that lets it
improve fast, and an incumbent margin structure that makes responding
irrational until too late.

**Output format:** the two-trajectory read in prose (where each sits today,
crossing horizon estimate with stated basis), the incumbents' likely
response and why their economics permit or forbid it, and a verdict:
*sustaining innovation / low-end disruption / new-market disruption / not
disruption* — with the So-what for the client's move.

## Three Horizons

**When:** portfolio and investment-mix decisions; placing a new bet in
context; annual planning.

**How:** H1 = defend and extend the core (returns now), H2 = scale emerging
businesses (returns in 1–3 years), H3 = options on futures (small, staged,
killed cheaply). Sort current initiatives and the proposal by *actual
resource allocation* — money and senior attention, not labels.

**Output format:** table — `Initiative | Horizon | Investment/yr [S#] |
Senior attention (L/M/H) | Status`, totals per horizon as % of investment,
then the imbalance read (typical failure: 95% H1, starving the future — or
H3 bets funded like H1 commitments).

**Tips:** an H3 bet is an option — its budget buys information, not revenue;
judge it by what it can teach cheaply. And place disruptive initiatives
*outside* the core's processes and metrics, or the core's immune system
(resources-processes-values) will kill them while everyone applauds the
strategy.
